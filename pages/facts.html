Origins of the Word Pork

Have you ever wondered why the names for animals in English is different from the words we use to refer to the meat we eat? This can be traced back to the Norman Conquest of Britain in the 11th century! Animals in the stables and in the farms kept their Old English names (like pig, cow, and sheep). However, when the animals were cooked and served to the French-speaking upper-class, an anglicized version of the French words were used. "Porc" became "pork", "beouf" became "beef", and "mouton" became "mutton." The lower-class Anglo-Saxons were the hunters, and therefore used the old words to refer to the animals. The upper-class French saw the animals only during mealtimes, and used the French word to describe the prepared dishes. That's why the word's are different! Source

Al Pastor

Tacos al pastor were created in 1930 in Puebla, Mexico by Lebanese immigrants who introduced the region to classic shawarma. They would serve the shawarma (roast lamb cooked on a vertical grill) on pita bread or flour tortillas. Originally known as tacos árabes, this dish eventually evolved into tacos al pastor when the local Mexicans incorporated their own spice blends into the marinade. Source

Meat-Shaped Stone 肉形石

This is unique piece of jasper carved into the shape of a piece of Dongpo pork, a popular Chinese way of preparing pork belly. It is on display at the National Palace Museum in Taipei, Taiwan. Alongside the Jadeite Cabbage and Mao Gong Ding, the Meat-Shaped Stone is recognized as the most important item in the museum's collection. The stone was carved from banded jasper during the Qing dynasty (1636 - 1912). Its many layers accumulated naturally over the years, and the artisan who carved the stone stained the skin, resulting in a realistic look of multiple layers of fat and meat. Source
